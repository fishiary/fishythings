Section 01

Your water: just how hard is 37 °fH?

Switzerland has beautifully clean tap water — but hardness varies enormously depending on the geology underfoot. Liestal sits on limestone-heavy Jura geology, and the water table reflects that. Your supply comes in at roughly 37 °fH (French degrees of hardness), which translates to about 22 °dH (German degrees) or 370 mg/litre of calcium carbonate.

You are not imagining the limescale. You are not being unlucky. At 37 °fH, every litre of water that passes through your pipes, your appliances, your taps, and your shower carries enough dissolved calcium that — when it evaporates or is heated — it leaves a measurable mineral deposit behind. Over 16 years and tens of thousands of litres, that adds up to what you've been living with.

To put this in personal terms: you grew up in Rathgar, Dublin, where the city supply runs at roughly 25–32 °fH — hard enough to know what limescale is, but noticeably less aggressive than Liestal. Your brother-in-law in Urdorf (Canton Zürich) is on approximately 15–20 °fH — he notices occasional scale but nothing like what you face. Your parents-in-law in Obfelden (Canton Zürich, Säuliamt) are on roughly 22–28 °fH from local groundwater — similar to Rathgar, familiar territory. And Zürich city residents at 10–14 °fH barely know what a limescale problem is, drawing from soft alpine and lake sources. Liestal is the hardest water of anywhere in your personal geography — harder than London's famously hard supply at ~30–35 °fH.

Why heat makes it so much worse. Calcium carbonate solubility decreases sharply with temperature — the opposite of most substances. Above 60°C it precipitates aggressively. This is why your solar thermal tank, TMV, oil burner heat exchanger, dishwasher heating element and kettle suffer far more than your cold pipes.

The bathroom: daily annoyance, real hygiene concern, and a losing battle

Of everything on this list, the bathroom situation is perhaps the most wearing — because it's every single day, it's visible, and no amount of effort fully fixes it. At 37 °fH, every shower deposits a microscopically thin calcium film on every surface the water touches. This sounds harmless. Over weeks and months, it isn't.

On grout — which is porous and slightly rough — calcium embeds into the surface texture and cannot be scrubbed out. You've already partially regrouted one bathroom, roughly seven years ago. That's a half-day of disruptive, expensive work driven entirely by hard water mineralisation. On the current trajectory, you're heading back there again.

The shower curtain accumulates a stiff, crusty calcified waterline that no washing cycle removes. The folds trap soap scum and the crystalline calcium surface gives bacteria exactly the foothold they need. Which brings us to the orange.

🟠

That orange-pink slime is a real hygiene issue — not just an aesthetic one

The orange-pink biofilm that reappears within days of cleaning is Serratia marcescens — a bacteria, not a mould — that thrives in damp bathroom environments. What makes it so persistent in your bathrooms specifically is that limescale gives it a scaffold. The rough, mineralised surface of calcified grout and a crusty shower curtain provides grip that smooth surfaces simply don't. On soft-water surfaces, Serratia has nowhere to anchor; it wipes away easily and returns slowly. On your current surfaces, it's back within days because the calcium structure never goes away. In healthy adults it's mostly harmless, but it's a genuine hygiene concern — and a significant source of daily cleaning frustration.

Your cleaner spends an estimated 25 minutes per bathroom visit on limescale-related work — scrubbing grout, treating taps, tackling the curtain and toilet surfaces. That's roughly CHF 625 per year in cleaning labour fighting a battle that cannot be won without addressing the root cause. (Assumption: 25 min/week at CHF 30/hr, 52 weeks/year.)

This is the single strongest quality-of-life argument for softening. The appliance damage is financially significant. But the bathroom situation is what you live with every day — the grout that never looks clean, the curtain that never feels fresh, the orange that returns before the week is out. Softened water doesn't just slow this down. It largely stops it.

Section 03

A safety concern you may not have considered

⚠️

Your thermostatic mixing valve needs attention now — regardless of what you decide about softening

The device in your basement that blends solar-hot water with cold water to deliver a safe household temperature is called a thermostatic mixing valve (TMV). Yours is over 10 years old, operating on 37 °fH water at temperatures up to 90°C from the solar collectors. You've noticed temperature inconsistency from your taps and showers, and you've been manually loosening the knob to free limescale blockages. These are textbook signs of a valve whose internal thermostatic element is compromised.

The TMV is one of the most limescale-vulnerable components in any house, because it combines the two worst conditions for scaling: high temperature and constant flow. Your solar system pushes this further — water hitting the valve can be at 80–90°C, which causes aggressive and rapid calcium precipitation right inside the valve mechanism.

A failing TMV has two serious failure modes:

Fails toward hot: Household hot water runs hotter than set — scalding risk, particularly for children or anyone showering.

Fails toward cold: Water temperature drops below 55°C — creating conditions where Legionella bacteria can multiply in standing water. Swiss SVGW guidelines exist precisely to prevent this.

Our recommendation: Have a plumber inspect and likely replace your TMV soon — this is independent of the softener decision. A replacement valve costs CHF 300–600 plus installation. If you do install a softener, the new TMV installed in softened water will last many decades. If you don't, budget to replace it again in another 8–10 years.

Section 04

Hard water, skin and hair: what the evidence actually says

After 16 years of daily showers at 37 °fH, there's a reasonable question worth asking: has your water been affecting how your skin and hair feel, not just how your bathroom looks? The short answer is: probably yes, and the mechanism is well understood even if the evidence is more robust for clinical skin conditions than for healthy adults.

What happens when you shower in hard water

The core problem is chemistry. Calcium and magnesium ions in hard water react with the surfactants in soap and shampoo to form insoluble compounds — the same soap scum that coats your shower walls. This reaction has two consequences. First, it reduces the effectiveness of your soap, meaning you need more of it to achieve a lather. Second, and more importantly, the resulting soap scum doesn't rinse away cleanly. The same residue that coats your tiles is, in effect, what's left on your skin and hair after every shower.

Calcium and magnesium form a mildly alkaline solution — and skin is normally mildly acidic. This shift in pH can compromise the skin's barrier function. A compromised barrier means skin loses moisture more easily and is more vulnerable to environmental irritants. The result, for many people, is the familiar sensation of tightness after showering — reaching for moisturiser before you've even dried off.

The eczema evidence — real, but nuanced

The most rigorous research concerns atopic eczema, and the findings are clear in direction if not absolute in magnitude. A systematic review and meta-analysis covering nearly 386,000 participants found meaningfully increased odds of atopic eczema in children exposed to harder versus softer water. The mechanism proposed is plausible: skin sites washed with hard water showed significantly increased deposits of sodium lauryl sulphate — a common soap surfactant — which in turn increased transepidermal water loss and caused skin irritation. In other words, hard water traps the very cleaning agents that are supposed to rinse away, leaving them in contact with skin where they cause damage over time.

However, the same body of research also found something important: randomised controlled trials comparing water softeners with standard care did not show a significant difference in objective eczema severity in children who already had the condition. So the association with developing eczema is real; softening water does not appear to cure established eczema. The evidence here is genuinely mixed, and it would be dishonest to overstate it.

For healthy skin — widely experienced, less rigorously proven

For adults without eczema or skin conditions, the peer-reviewed evidence is thinner. What exists is directionally consistent: hard water interferes with soap rinsing, leaves residue, and disrupts the skin's moisture balance. But large controlled trials on healthy adult skin are sparse. What fills that gap is self-reported experience — and here, the consistency across households is striking.

People who switch to softened water almost universally report the same things: skin feels less tight immediately after showering, less moisturiser is needed, and hair rinses more cleanly. The silkier, almost slightly slippery feeling of soft water on skin is unfamiliar at first — it lacks the "squeaky clean" sensation many people associate with a good shower. That squeaky feeling, it turns out, is not clean skin. It is soap scum and mineral deposits on the skin surface. Soft water produces a genuinely cleaner result; the sensation just takes a short adjustment.

The moisturiser question. If you or anyone in your household reaches for body lotion immediately after every shower, it is worth asking whether this is a personal skin type issue or a 37 °fH issue. After 16 years, it can be genuinely difficult to tell. Most households that switch to soft water find they use significantly less body lotion, face cream and hand cream — because the water is no longer stripping moisture and leaving soap residue in its place.

Hair

The same mechanism applies to hair. Hard water interferes with shampoo — requiring more product to achieve a lather, then failing to rinse shampoo and conditioner away completely. This leaves a mineral and product buildup that makes hair feel dry, look dull and become harder to manage. Colour-treated hair is particularly affected, as mineral deposits interact with dye molecules and accelerate colour fading.

What this means for your household

None of your family are known eczema sufferers, so the clinical evidence is less directly relevant. But 16 years of daily showering at 37 °fH is a long time for soap residue and mineral film to be the chronic baseline. The dry skin feeling after showering, the need for body lotion before you feel comfortable, the hair that doesn't quite behave — these are not imagined. They are the predictable result of what hard water does to soap chemistry, every single day.

Softening to 5–8 °fH is not a medical intervention. But the quality-of-life shift reported by households making this change is consistently positive — and for a household that has been living with 37 °fH for 16 years, the contrast would likely be noticeable within the first week.

Section 06

The environmental question: salt, water, conscience

Your concern about salt use is legitimate and worth taking seriously — not dismissing. Here's an honest assessment.

Salt discharge into wastewater

Ion-exchange softeners work by flushing hardness minerals off a resin bed using a brine (salt water) solution, which is then discharged to drain. For a 4-person household at 37 °fH, this means roughly 120–180 kg of salt per year entering the wastewater system.

Switzerland's wastewater treatment infrastructure handles this well — sodium chloride passes through treatment plants largely unchanged, but Swiss rivers and groundwater do accumulate some additional sodium load. This is a real, if modest, environmental impact. It's worth acknowledging rather than hand-waving away.

The counterargument that's often missed: Consider what you're currently doing without a softener. You're using descaling chemicals — citric acid, sulphamic acid — regularly on the tank, washing machine, dishwasher and other appliances. These also go to drain. You're replacing appliances significantly earlier than their design life, which has a large embodied carbon and resource cost. A washing machine or dishwasher replaced 5–8 years early represents far more environmental impact than several years of salt discharge.

Water use for regeneration — and how it works

Modern demand-controlled softeners have a water meter built in. They count every litre that passes through and calculate exactly when the resin bed is exhausted, based on your actual consumption and your inlet hardness. When regeneration is needed, it triggers automatically — typically in the early hours of the morning (2–4am) when nobody is using water. You wake up to freshly regenerated resin with no involvement whatsoever.

For a 4-person household at 37 °fH, this works out to roughly one to two regeneration cycles per week. Each regeneration uses approximately 50–80 litres of water — comparable to one toilet flush cycle — and produces a brine discharge to the drain. That's roughly 5,000–8,000 extra litres per year, about 1–2% of a typical household's annual water consumption. In Switzerland's water-abundant context, this is not a meaningful concern.

The only human task involved is topping up the salt hopper every 6–8 weeks: a 25 kg bag from Landi or Migros Bau+Hobby at around CHF 8–12. The whole top-up takes about two minutes.

Mitigation options — what each setting means in real terms

If salt discharge is important to you, two approaches help: demand-controlled regeneration (only regenerates when needed — look for this feature explicitly) and setting the softener to partial hardness rather than zero. Most Swiss plumbers recommend setting to 5–8 °fH rather than 0 — this dramatically reduces salt use while still preventing scale formation.

Section 07

Drinking water: sodium, taste, and the bypass tap

You drink tap water daily — as any Swiss household should. Here's what softening actually means for your drinking water, and whether you need to do anything about it.

Ion-exchange softening works by replacing calcium and magnesium ions with sodium ions. The resulting water contains more sodium than unsoftened water. At 37 °fH fully softened to 0 °fH, the sodium addition is approximately 115 mg per litre. At the recommended setting of 5–8 °fH, it's roughly 25–35 mg/litre extra — a modest increase that stays well within WHO guidelines of 200 mg/litre and Swiss drinking water standards.

To put this in context: according to the Swiss Salt Study 2022–23, Swiss adults consume an average of around 9–10 grams of salt per day — predominantly from processed foods, bread, cheese and cooked meals. For a 4-person household, that's roughly 36–40 grams of sodium chloride per day consumed through food and cooking. How much does the softener add on top of that? At the recommended 5–8 °fH residual setting, drinking and cooking water adds approximately 25–35 mg of sodium per litre. Assuming each person drinks and cooks with 1.5–2 litres of tap water daily, the whole household consumes perhaps 150–280 mg of extra sodium per day from softened water — roughly 0.4–0.7% of the household's daily dietary sodium intake. This is well below the threshold of measurement error in any dietary study. The softener is, in sodium terms, a rounding error compared to a single slice of bread.

For a healthy family with teenagers, this is not a health concern. Where a bypass tap becomes more important is for:

  • Preparing infant formula (WHO recommends unsoftened water — not relevant for teenagers)
  • People on clinician-advised low-sodium diets
  • Anyone who strongly prefers the mineral taste of hard water

For your household: a bypass tap is optional, not essential. At 5–8 °fH residual and with healthy teenagers, the sodium addition is nutritionally insignificant. Softened water at this setting tastes slightly silkier than hard water but not noticeably salty. If you're not fussy about the taste difference, you don't need one. That said, a kitchen bypass tap costs only CHF 150–300 to install at the same time as the softener — if there's any chance you'd want the option later, it's cheap insurance to include it from the start.

On cooking: some bakers notice softened water slightly affects dough (gluten development is subtly different with lower mineral content), and pasta cooked in softened water tastes marginally different to some. These are minor effects at 5–8 °fH residual and most households don't notice them.

Section 08

Salt-free alternatives: the technology, the debate, and whether they work for your situation

The report so far has focused exclusively on ion-exchange softeners — the salt-based kind. That's deliberate, but before you approach suppliers there is a genuine industry debate worth understanding honestly. Not because it changes the conclusion for your situation, but because you will encounter these alternatives in conversations with installers, and the marketing around some of them is aggressive enough to warrant a clear-eyed look.

The technologies

The credible salt-free approaches fall into two families.

Biomineralisation (template-assisted crystallisation) — the approach used by JRG Coral Force, made by Georg Fischer (Schaffhausen). Catalyst granules trigger dissolved calcium and carbonate ions to nucleate into micro-crystals, which stay suspended in the water and flush through the system rather than depositing on pipe walls and heat exchangers. No salt, no sodium addition, no regeneration water, no electricity, no service contract. Cartridge replacement every one to three years. This is serious Swiss engineering from a major industrial manufacturer, and it is standard specification in a significant proportion of Swiss new-builds where building technology engineers are involved in the design.

Acid-binding cartridge systems — the approach used by Evodrop (Thurgau). A cartridge containing malic acid granules claims to bind calcium ions before they can precipitate as scale. The company cites a 94% limescale protection rate from a DVGW-accredited laboratory test.

There is also a long tail of technologies not worth considering for a whole-house application at 37 °fH: magnetic conditioners, electronic descalers, polyphosphate dosers. These are not serious contenders, and independent Swiss testing has confirmed they have no meaningful descaling effect.

On Evodrop specifically

Evodrop deserves more scrutiny than its marketing invites. The company is prominent in Swiss Google results — conspicuously so — and the gap between the claims on their website and independently verifiable evidence is wide enough to notice.

Their website declares Evodrop the "personal test winner" for Swiss descaling systems, then explains in the same breath: "The short answer: No, there is no official test winner in Switzerland. Why? Because there have been no comprehensive, neutral tests for descaling systems to date." They have awarded themselves a prize in a competition that does not exist. The 94% protection rate comes from a single laboratory test the company commissioned; there is no long-term independent field data at high hardness published by a neutral party.

The website also runs an extended attack on salt-based systems — sodium content, environmental damage, "hazardous waste from exchange resins" — that is disproportionate and in places misleading. Exhausted ion-exchange resin is not classified as hazardous waste under Swiss regulations. The sodium addition at a 5–8 °fH residual setting is nutritionally insignificant for a healthy household. The framing reads less like engineering scepticism and more like competitive positioning from a company that needs to differentiate itself from a hundred-year-old, well-proven technology.

None of this means the malic acid chemistry is fraudulent — it is a real approach with a plausible mechanism. But for a 37 °fH household making a CHF 3,000–4,000 infrastructure decision, "we declared ourselves the winner of a non-existent test" is not the confidence-building statement the company seems to think it is.

The legitimate professional debate

The disagreement between salt-based and salt-free approaches is genuine and not simply marketing noise. Building technology engineers — particularly those focused on energy systems, heating infrastructure and environmental sustainability — often favour salt-free conditioning on principled grounds. Salt discharge into the wastewater system is a real environmental cost. Ion exchange permanently removes calcium and magnesium from the water, adding sodium in their place; some engineers regard this as an unnecessary chemical intervention in a water supply that was fine to begin with. Salt-free systems, when they work, are lower maintenance and produce no brine discharge.

One housing technology professional consulted during the preparation of this report — with direct experience specifying JRG Coral Force on Swiss residential projects and with the system installed in their own home — expressed principled scepticism about salt-based softening and genuine confidence in biomineralisation for infrastructure protection. That is a credible position held by credible people, and it should be taken seriously.

Where the debate hits a wall for your situation

The professional disagreement has a specific and important limit: both biomineralisation and acid-binding systems leave the calcium in the water. They change its form — converting dissolved ions into micro-crystals or bound complexes that resist depositing inside pipes and heat exchangers. What they cannot do is remove calcium from water before it reaches an open surface and evaporates.

This is the crux. The bathroom problem — the grout degradation, the crusted shower curtain, the taps that pit and frost, and most of all the Serratia marcescens biofilm that returns within days of cleaning — is not caused by calcium depositing inside pipes. It is caused by calcium-rich water evaporating on surfaces and leaving a mineral film. Salt-free conditioned water still contains all the calcium that Liestal's 37 °fH supply does. It arrives in a different form, but it arrives. It evaporates. It deposits. The scaffold remains.

When the housing technology professional mentioned above was pressed specifically on the bathroom surface and biofilm problem at 37 °fH — not the heating infrastructure, the daily bathroom reality — the honest answer was that this is precisely where salt-free systems fall short.

The assessment

JRG Coral Force is serious Swiss engineering and is worth understanding as a technology. It will protect your heating infrastructure well — boiler, TMV, solar tank, oil burner heat exchanger. The manufacturer is credible and the technology is real.

But it will not clean up your bathrooms. It will not stop the orange from coming back. It will not extend the life of your grout. And at 37 °fH — the hardest water in your personal geography — the calcium load hitting every bathroom surface every day is not a problem that conditioning alone can solve.

The question to sit with. If the bathroom problem were not on your list — if your only concerns were the boiler, the solar tank, and the TMV — would ion exchange still be justified? Possibly not. JRG Coral Force would be a rational, lower-maintenance, salt-free choice for protecting the heating infrastructure. But the bathroom problem is on your list, and it's one of your top two daily frustrations. That changes the answer.

Swiss brands, suppliers & what the reviews really say

The Swiss market is well-served for water softeners — this is a country with widespread hard water and high domestic standards. But not all suppliers are equal, and the most important thing to understand upfront is this: the technology is standard across all reputable brands. What you're really buying is the service relationship. Price differences of 2× for equivalent specs are common, as confirmed by Swiss forum users who shared side-by-side quote comparisons.

The following is based on independent Swiss forum data (haus-forum.ch), Trustpilot, and customer testimonials — not manufacturer marketing material.

Salt: is there a proprietary lock-in? (Short answer: no — for Grünbeck)

A reasonable concern. Grünbeck's softliQ range uses completely standard regeneration salt tablets — the same DIN EN 973 Typ A tablets you can buy in a 25 kg bag from Landi or OBI for around CHF 8–10. No proprietary format, no lock-in. Grünbeck sells their own-brand version called Regenit, which is the same standard tablet salt in Grünbeck packaging — you can ignore it and buy generic.

The "proprietary" shape you may have heard about is most likely BWT's Perlatabs — BWT's own tablet format. Any EN 973-compliant tablet salt works in BWT hardware too, but BWT's service literature nudges you toward their own product. Worth knowing about if you go that route.

The broader salt rule: All mainstream ion-exchange softeners run on standard DIN EN 973 Typ A tablet salt. Buy it in 25 kg bags from Landi (~CHF 8–10/bag) or Migros Bau+Hobby. One thing to confirm with your installer: a small number of older or budget devices use fine granular salt rather than tablets — these are not interchangeable. Ask your installer to confirm the salt format before you buy the first bag.

Before you start: the one-off Durgol bathroom reset

Once soft water is flowing, limescale stops accumulating — but the 16 years already deposited won't disappear on their own. Plan a one-off deep descale of bathroom surfaces within the first week, then let the soft water do the maintenance from that point forward.

You've learned from experience that undiluted Durgol dissolves grout. Here's the safe approach:

  • Dilute 1 part Durgol Universal to 3–4 parts water — never apply undiluted to tiled surfaces
  • Apply with a soft sponge or brush; leave for no more than 2–3 minutes
  • Rinse very thoroughly
  • Repeat if needed rather than increasing concentration
  • Test coloured or darker grout on a small hidden area first
  • Safe on standard ceramic and porcelain tiles at this dilution

Taps and chrome fittings can handle undiluted Durgol for short periods (30–60 seconds), but rinse promptly and don't leave it pooling. After this one-off reset, maintaining the bathrooms with soft water and a weekly light clean should keep surfaces genuinely clean rather than merely surface-clean over a hidden mineral layer.

Section 11

Hotel bathrooms: what actually makes them look immaculate

You've noticed it on holidays and business trips: Swiss hotel bathrooms gleam. No orange slime in the grout. Taps that shine. You come home to Liestal and within a week the bathroom looks tired again. It's natural to wonder whether hotels are doing something with their water that you're not.

The honest answer is: partly, but probably not in the way the water softener industry would like you to believe. This is a claim worth examining carefully rather than repeating uncritically.

What hotels actually do

Hotels in hard-water areas do generally treat their water — but the primary driver is infrastructure protection, not bathroom aesthetics. Industrial laundry equipment, dishwashers and boilers running around the clock at 30+ °fH would require constant descaling and early replacement without some form of treatment. The economics are straightforward. What technology they use — ion exchange, biomineralisation, or a combination — varies by property and is not standardised across the Swiss hotel industry. It is not accurate to say that "most Swiss hotels soften their water"; verifiable data on this simply isn't available.

Why hotel bathrooms really look good

Professional cleaning discipline is the primary reason, and it is not trivially replicable at home. Hotel housekeepers are trained to squeegee shower screens after every clean — removing water from the surface before it can evaporate and deposit calcium. They turn over bathrooms daily, which means biofilm like Serratia marcescens never gets the 48–72 hours it needs to establish itself. They use professional-grade acidic cleaners significantly stronger than retail Viakal or Durgol. And they almost always have glass screens rather than curtains — glass squeegeed clean every day is a fundamentally easier surface to maintain than fabric that accumulates calcified scale in its folds.

None of these things require softened water. A bathroom fed with hard water and cleaned daily with a squeegee and professional products will look dramatically better than one cleaned weekly without them. The water quality matters — but the cleaning discipline matters at least as much.

The practical takeaway for your household. Whether or not you install a softener, adopting two hotel habits would compound any improvement significantly: squeegee shower surfaces after every use (takes 20 seconds), and replace the shower curtain with a glass screen if you haven't already. With soft water and these habits, your bathrooms would stay genuinely clean between cleans rather than requiring a battle each time. Without soft water, these habits help — but they're fighting a harder battle.
